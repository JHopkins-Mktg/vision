Strong top-of-funnel. Inconsistent revenue.

The Buildout marketing organization is generating a healthy and growing volume of inbound leads. But the Executive Team has aligned on a concern: lead quality and downstream revenue performance are inconsistent despite strong top-of-funnel results.

  • Lead volume is increasing quarter-over-quarter

01
Question One

How I'd assess the health of the funnel.

The health of the funnel is really focused around how well it drives revenue.

Core thesis

When I come into any new role, the first move is to define what we're measuring — and then audit the system against it.

"First things first — how are we defining a lead?" I personally define a lead as someone who raises their hand to see the product or talk to sales. Everything else is intent data — which is useful, but it requires further marketing to get to a hand raise. Conflate the two and every downstream metric blurs.

Other areas I audit immediately:

01

Marketing revenue vs. target

How well are we actually driving revenue — not just delivering leads? Pipeline that doesn't close isn't pipeline.

Break out by channel and product
02

Pipeline velocity

What are the stages, their definitions, and stage-to-stage conversion rates? Is everything flowing — or is there a bottleneck?

Break out by channel and product
03

MQL qualification rate

Of the leads we're bringing in, how many are truly qualified — meaning they could buy from us now or in the future?

Break out by channel and product
04

SQL qualification rate

Of those leads, how many have a need now, pass BANT, and convert to an opportunity?

Break out by channel and product
05

Average deal size

What's the budgeted deal size for the year, and how well are we hitting it?

Break out by channel and product
06

Sales cycle length

How long is it taking from hand-raise to revenue?

Break out by channel and product
07

Sales × marketing contribution

How does sales-contributed pipeline compare to marketing-contributed pipeline? If one team has higher conversions, deal sizes, or velocity, that's a knowledge-sharing opportunity — not a finger-pointing one.

That audit gives me the shape of the problem. From there, the fixes get specific.

02
Question Two

How I'd improve lead quality without stalling growth.

Adding criteria doesn't stall growth — it accelerates it. You know what drives revenue, and you stop wasting motion on the rest.

The counter-intuitive truth

Again — my opinion is that lead quality starts with a true definition of a lead.

From there, it's all about being vigilant on your ICP and the internal SLAs that define an MQL. Every demand-gen campaign should be leveraging specific targeting and curated data sets to support the revenue targets to this specific buyer.

Example MQL definition for Buildout

+

A lead becomes an MQL when all of the following are true:

  • Raises their hand to see the product or talk to sales
  • Company is a commercial real estate brokerage (verified via ZoomInfo / Clearbit enrichment or domain match)
  • Company size: 5+ licensed brokers OR $5M+ annual transaction volume
  • Geography: US or Canada
  • Not currently a Buildout customer on the relevant product (cross-referenced against CRM)
  • Not flagged as a competitor employee or analyst (firmographic disqualifier list)

The instinct is to think tighter qualification slows growth. The opposite is true. When you know what drives revenue, you spend less time on what doesn't — and you get there faster.

Customer Leads · Same Rule Applies

For existing customer expansion, this strategy still holds. Build out an ICP customer matrix across products and bake that targeting into every expansion and cross-sell campaign. Count them as a lead the moment they raise their hand to see additional functionality or meet with the AM team — not before.

Question Three

How I'd demonstrate revenue impact.

The best way to demonstrate revenue impact is to deliver money to the table.

The thesis

Build a pipeline that doesn't just drive interest — one that converts into recurring revenue.

Here's how I think about it:

01 · Forecast
Build the forecast

Historic benchmarks inform initial assumptions. Targets are grounded in data, not aspiration.

02 · Diagnose
Identify gaps & accelerants

Where in the existing marketing mix are the gaps? Where are the unlocks?

03 · Strategize
Set the strategy

Marketing plan that ladders directly to the promised number — by channel, segment, and product.

04 · Instrument
Build the infrastructure

Right systems, right tagging, right dashboards. Measure performance meticulously from day one.

05 · Execute
Hit GO

Then turn the knobs based on the data — adjusting in real time to hit target.

From there it's discipline: read the numbers, adjust the inputs, and stay accountable to the forecast.

Past Example

Same problem, different industry. Rev.io — billing, PSA, RMM and payments for service providers.

When the leads weren't really leads — they just showed intent.

— The situation

Inflated lead definitions.

The previous team was inserting tradeshow attendee lists as leads. That inflated total volume and blurred every metric downstream — making it nearly impossible to zone in on what was actually driving revenue.

Conversion rates looked artificially low. Cycle times looked artificially long.
— What I did

Recalibrated the definition.

Reset the internal definition of a lead and explained the why to the org. Restructured how everything was tagged in Salesforce — including marketing source and lead stage, which weren't being captured properly.

A single shared definition. A trustworthy data layer.
— The outcome

Faster, cleaner pipeline.

With the intent data filtered out, we had a hyper-focused view into which channels drove true hand-raises and how fast they moved through the funnel. Same activity, sharper picture. Delivered pipeline faster.

Marketing started reporting in revenue terms — and was believed.

Diagnose the funnel. Define the lead. Build the revenue engine. Then earn the brand.

Market Reality

Buildout leads the category — in a market crowded with AI-first claims.

Buildout is viewed as a leader in CRE automation. But the market is increasingly crowded with AI-first claims. Buyers are excited yet skeptical, making trust and credibility critical.

Brand isn't a campaign. It's how we sound, what we show, who we are when challenged — and whether the proof matches the promise.

01
Question One

How I'd sharpen Buildout's brand positioning.

Our homepage is our billboard. Prospects want to see the product — not read words about it.

The core principle

Buildout offers a comprehensive, end-to-end platform. We have to speak to the depth of what we offer — while showing how seamless we've made it to work across workflows.

That starts with how we talk. Speak in layman's terms. Don't lead with the what — lead with the why. Brokers don't buy "an end-to-end CRE platform." They buy faster deals, cleaner pipelines, and more time selling. That's the story.

Then there's how we show up. The homepage is our billboard — it's where the strongest positioning has to live. Four structural moves:

01

Dial up the product

Show how we seamlessly execute across each workflow. Visual proof beats written claims every time. Prospects want to see the platform in action.

02

Lean into market-leader language

"One platform, one login. From the team that's powered 50,000+ brokers through 15 years of CRE evolution."

03

Restructure the top nav

Site structure is always an easy win. The top nav is where we own the narrative — instead of letting prospects assume what we offer.

04

Lead with the why

Strip the jargon. Layman's terms only. Every section answers: what does this do for me, this week?

02
You don't get ankle-biters until you're the leg worth biting. Question Two

How I'd handle the ankle-biters.

Everyone is running comparison pages on Buildout. Return the favor — and crush the competitors actually impacting your revenue.

The strategy

We offer multiple solutions — so we have multiple ankle-biters. Step one is figuring out which ones are worth our time.

Prioritization Framework

Two filters. Who's encroaching on market share, and who's actually stealing dollars from us. Prioritize accordingly.

Encroachment Signals Bidding on Buildout brand keywords. Sponsoring our key industry events. Showing up in earned media against us.
Revenue Signals CRM competitive-loss data. Pipeline lost to a named competitor. Switcher conversations from CS.

Once we've named the priority list, here's what runs against them:

Play 01

SEO comparison pages

Everyone is doing this to Buildout. Return the favor. Ship dedicated comparison pages for the priority ankle-biters — honest, structured, customer-centric. We own the SERP for our own brand.

Foundational
Play 02

Flag Google for ™ infringement

Competitors aren't allowed to use the Buildout brand name in their ad headlines. Submit takedown requests. Usually a fast way to get these ads pulled and recapture branded traffic.

Quick win
Play 03

Battlecards

Equip sales to shut down competitive comparisons in real time. One per priority competitor. Tactical, current, and tied to objections heard in actual calls — not generic feature lists.

Foundational
Play 04

Switcher case studies

Gather testimonials from customers who came to Buildout because a competitor couldn't perform. Use them as ammo in campaigns, on comparison pages, and in sales decks.

Past Example

Rev.io — repositioning a dated telecom-billing platform into an all-in-one platform for service providers.

A delicate balance: bring legacy customers forward — without losing them.

— The setup

A brand stuck in the past.

Rev.io was perceived as a dated, telecom communications billing platform. The product had evolved — now an all-in-one platform for service providers delivering billing, PSA, RMM, and payments — but the brand still spoke to a smaller, narrower category.

Legacy customers stable. New growth tier locked out by old positioning.
— The work

Repositioned alongside the product.

Brought legacy customers into the new era of the brand without losing them. Simultaneously repositioned the offering to entice new customers to the full platform. Every touchpoint — homepage, sales deck, content — re-engineered around the broader value.

A brand wide enough for the new product. Narrow enough for the old customer.
— The outcome

New revenue. Stickier customers.

Done properly, repositioning introduces multiple revenue streams for the business and increases retention and expansion due to product stickiness. The same playbook fits Buildout's evolution: from Showcase to the full CRE operating system.

M&A activity, new product launches, and brand evolution have been a through-line in every role.

Question Four

How I'd market AI without overpromising.

You can't fake this. Either you have a respectable AI offering or you don't. The product has to stand on its own.

The honest truth

People want to see AI working. Not hear you talk about it.

That's the entire game. So the marketing job becomes: stop talking, start showing.

◆ The defensible asset

Buildout sits on an incredible amount of CRE data that competitors do not have. That data should be the foundation of the AI architecture — and the centerpiece of how we market it.

The story practically writes itself: "We know CRE's workflow. We've measured a significant amount of data points for over 15 years and that's given us a proprietary data layer unique to the market."

From there, the proof is in two specific demonstrations:

Show the lift
This used to take you 2 days.
Now it's done in two clicks.
Show the comparison
Generic AI promises from competitors.
A side-by-side that proves the difference.

Every AI feature needs a "before and after" moment and a comparable benchmark. No vapor messaging. No roadmap features marketed as shipped. The credibility is the differentiator — and Buildout already has the data, the customers, and the 15-year track record to back it up.
